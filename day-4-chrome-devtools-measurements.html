How to Run a Lighthouse Audit — Step by Step
🔦

Running Your First Lighthouse Report

1

Open the website you want to test

Go to daraz.pk, amazon.com, or youtube.com in Chrome.

2

Open DevTools → Click "Lighthouse" tab

Press F12 then find the Lighthouse tab at the top of DevTools (you may need to click >> to see more tabs).

3

Configure your audit settings

Select Device: Mobile and check Performance category. For research consistency, always use Mobile mode.

4

Click "Analyze page load" and wait

Lighthouse will reload the page, record everything for ~60 seconds, then generate your report automatically.

5

Record your metrics from the report

Write down your LCP, FCP, CLS, INP scores. Take a screenshot of the full report for your research records.

Metric Reference Guide
LCP
Largest Contentful Paint
How fast does the main content (hero image or big text) appear? This is what users visually notice first.
Good: < 2.5s
OK: 2.5 – 4.0s
Poor: > 4.0s
FCP
First Contentful Paint
When does ANY content first appear? Even a spinner counts. Tells users "something is loading."
Good: < 1.8s
OK: 1.8 – 3.0s
Poor: > 3.0s
CLS
Cumulative Layout Shift
Does content jump around while loading? High CLS = frustrating experience (buttons moving as you click).
Good: < 0.1
OK: 0.1 – 0.25
Poor: > 0.25
INP
Interaction to Next Paint
How fast does the page respond when you tap or click? Critical for SPAs where JS handles all interactions.
Good: < 200ms
OK: 200–500ms
Poor: > 500ms

My Research Analysis — In My Own Words
📝

What the Data Shows

Today I collected my first real performance data from three major websites using Chrome Lighthouse. The results were surprising in some ways and confirmatory in others. daraz.pk, a traditional MPA-style e-commerce site, scored poorly on LCP (8.3s) — meaning the main product images load very slowly on mobile. However, its INP (180ms) was actually the best of the three, suggesting that server-side rendering makes individual interactions snappier.

amazon.com performed the best overall with a Lighthouse score of 52/100, the fastest FCP (1.4s), and an excellent CLS (0.06). Amazon engineers clearly invest heavily in performance optimization — they reserve exact image dimensions so the layout never shifts. This is a real-world example of good performance engineering.

youtube.com showed the clearest SPA trade-offs: the fastest visual response (FCP 1.6s — the JS shell appears instantly) but the worst CLS (0.31) and INP (540ms). This confirms what Day 2's paper taught me — SPAs feel fast at first but struggle with layout stability and interaction responsiveness under heavy JavaScript load.

💡

Key Research Insights from This Data

1. MPA vs SPA is not simply "one is better." daraz.pk (MPA) wins on INP. YouTube (SPA) wins on FCP. Amazon (hybrid) wins overall. The correct answer depends entirely on which metric you prioritize — and that depends on what your users need.

2. JavaScript file count correlates directly with poor INP. YouTube has 67 JS files and the worst INP (540ms). daraz.pk has 23 JS files and the best INP (180ms). This is a clear, data-supported pattern I can investigate further in my research.

3. Real-world data often contradicts theoretical predictions. I expected the MPA (daraz) to have the best LCP — but it was actually the worst, likely because of unoptimized images. This shows why measuring matters more than assuming.

4. These measurements are my research baseline. When I build my own MPA and SPA in later days, I will compare against these real-world benchmarks to see whether my implementations perform better or worse than production websites.

❓

Questions This Data Raised

Question 1: daraz.pk has an 8.3s LCP but is an MPA — which should theoretically have faster initial loads. Is this a failure of architecture, or a failure of implementation (unoptimized images, no CDN, slow server)? How do I separate "bad architecture" from "bad engineering" in my research measurements?

Question 2: YouTube's FCP is excellent (1.6s) because the SPA shell loads instantly — but the page is not usable until content fills in. Should FCP be weighted differently for SPAs where the initial "shell" is just a loading skeleton? This metric design question could be a contribution of my research.

Question 3: None of these sites let me test security directly through DevTools. How should I design a controlled security experiment that is parallel to this performance measurement approach?

📊 How My Understanding Evolved Today
🌅

Before Measuring — What I Assumed

✗

I assumed MPA websites would always load faster than SPA websites — because the theory says server-rendered HTML is faster.

✗

I thought Lighthouse scores for big companies like Amazon and YouTube would be very high — near 90 or 100 — because they have expert engineers.

✗

I assumed all four metrics (LCP, FCP, CLS, INP) would follow the same pattern for each website — if one is good, they'd all be good.

🌟

After Measuring — What I Now Know

✓

Architecture theory is only part of the story. Implementation quality (image optimization, CDN, caching) matters just as much. daraz.pk's poor LCP is likely an implementation failure, not an architecture failure.

✓

Even Amazon and YouTube score below 60/100 on Lighthouse mobile. Production performance is genuinely hard — and this makes my research relevant because even large companies have not "solved" this problem.

✓

The four metrics tell independent stories. YouTube has great FCP but terrible INP. daraz has terrible LCP but great INP. Each metric captures a distinct user experience dimension — all four are necessary for fair comparison.

Finding the Lighthouse tab in DevTools was slightly confusing at first — it is hidden behind the >> overflow menu on smaller screens. Running the audit on mobile took longer than expected (about 90 seconds per site). The hardest part was understanding why the scores were what they were — just seeing numbers is not enough.
