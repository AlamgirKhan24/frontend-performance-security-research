Section 1 — Research Overview
1

What is my research about?

My research focuses on frontend web application architectures — specifically comparing Multi-Page Applications (MPA) and Single-Page Applications (SPA). I will analyze both architectures across two critical dimensions: performance (page load speed, time-to-interactive, rendering efficiency) and security (XSS vulnerabilities, CSRF attacks, authentication risks). Using real-world benchmarks and controlled experiments, I aim to produce evidence-based guidance for developers choosing between these two approaches.

💡

Be specific about the TWO architectures (MPA vs SPA) and TWO dimensions (performance + security). This shows focused thinking to the committee.

2

Why does this research matter?

Web applications now power critical services in banking, healthcare, education, and e-commerce. The choice between MPA and SPA is one of the most consequential architectural decisions a developer makes, yet most guidance is based on opinion rather than data. Poor performance costs businesses revenue — a 1-second delay in page load reduces conversions by 7%. Poor security costs even more: data breaches average $4.45 million per incident globally. This research will fill the gap between anecdotal developer advice and empirical evidence.

💡

Ground your reasoning in real impact — business loss, security cost, or user impact. This shows the committee you think at scale.

3

What specific problem am I solving?

I am solving the architecture selection problem: when building a modern web application, developers face a critical choice between MPA and SPA without reliable comparative data on performance and security trade-offs. Current literature treats these architectures in isolation. My research will be the first systematic side-by-side comparison under controlled conditions, providing developers with a decision framework grounded in measurable evidence rather than developer preference.

💡

The key phrase is "decision framework" — you are not just measuring, you are producing something actionable and useful.

Section 2 — Impact & Expectations
4

Who will benefit from my research?

Three groups will benefit directly. First, frontend developers who need objective guidance when choosing a web architecture for new projects. Second, engineering managers and CTOs who make architectural decisions affecting performance budgets and security compliance. Third, the academic community — this research contributes a replicable methodology for evaluating web architectures. Indirectly, end users will benefit from faster, more secure products built on evidence-based decisions.

💡

Think in three layers: developers (technical), decision-makers (business), and end-users (human impact). This shows breadth of thinking.

5

What do I expect to find?

I expect MPA to outperform SPA on initial page load (First Contentful Paint, Time to First Byte) because server-rendered HTML requires no JavaScript bundle parsing. However, I expect SPA to deliver faster perceived performance after initial load due to client-side navigation. On security, I expect SPA to present a larger attack surface due to greater reliance on client-side JavaScript, making it more vulnerable to XSS attacks, while MPA may face greater CSRF risk. These are hypotheses my experiments will confirm or challenge.

💡

Use specific metric names (FCP, TTFB) and attack types (XSS, CSRF). This signals technical fluency to the admissions committee.
